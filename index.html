Alzheimer’s disease across EC, HC and PFC

A comparative project testing whether AD progression is organized as a continuous transcriptional trajectory or as a set of discrete disease-associated states. Initial hypothesis → tested → revised model

Initial hypothesis: AD progression can be represented as a continuous trajectory in transcriptional space, either globally or within region-specific branches.

What we tested

UMAP, PAGA, DPT pseudotime, root sensitivity, CellRank, state-based mapping, drivers and gene programs.

Why the trajectory model weakened

Pseudotime was root-dependent, Braak did not align consistently with ordering, PAGA was branching or fragmented, and CellRank did not reveal robust directed progression.

What the data support instead

Discrete disease-associated states with region-specific topology, cell-type composition and molecular programs.

Final model

AD is not one universal trajectory. Each region balances ordered structure, local state transitions and distinct molecular programs differently.

How DPT pseudotime and CellRank were interpreted

Two complementary methods were used to test whether AD-related transcriptional changes form a continuous progression-like structure.

DPT pseudotime

Diffusion pseudotime (DPT) orders cells along a neighborhood graph starting from a chosen root cell or root cluster. It is computed from diffusion distances on the graph and asks whether cells can be arranged along a continuous transcriptional path.

In a true trajectory-like system, pseudotime should remain relatively stable across reasonable root choices and should align with pathology measures such as Braak stage. In this project, pseudotime was often root-dependent and did not consistently match pathology, arguing against a robust continuous trajectory.

CellRank fate probabilities

CellRank estimates transition tendencies on the cell-state graph and computes the probability that each cell will end in predefined terminal states, such as low-pathology or high-pathology states.

If disease progression followed a strong directed trajectory, fate probabilities would form a coherent gradient toward pathological terminal states. Instead, our results showed locally distributed or weak fate biases without a stable global direction, supporting a state-based rather than trajectory-based interpretation.

Interpretation rule used throughout the dashboard: a continuous trajectory would require consistent ordering, stable directionality, and alignment with pathology. Local gradients or local fate biases alone were not considered sufficient evidence for a true disease trajectory.

EC — Entorhinal cortex

Most ordered regional structure, but trajectory evidence remains unstable under validation.
Detailed regional report

Step 1–2. Unsupervised Landscape and Disease Mapping

Clusters, pathology overlay, disease-associated states on the regional manifold, PAGA.

Unsupervised analysis of the entorhinal cortex reveals a well-structured transcriptional landscape with multiple distinct clusters. Mapping pathology onto this space highlights heterogeneous distributions of low, intermediate, and high pathology states across the manifold. PAGA topology captures partial connectivity between clusters, suggesting locally coherent transitions while preserving a fragmented global structure. Pathology signals appear unevenly distributed across connected components, without a clear monotonic pattern across the graph. Local patterns suggest potential relationships between clusters and disease burden, motivating further analysis of global organization and progression structure.

Step 3–4. Structure Validation and Model Choice

DPT root sensitivity testing and local branch testing were used to assess whether EC supports a continuous trajectory.

Pseudotime analysis suggests local gradients within subsets of clusters. However, these patterns do not extend into a coherent global trajectory across the manifold. Additionally, pseudotime does not consistently align with Braak staging, indicating a lack of a unified progression axis.

Diffusion pseudotime (DPT) was computed using multiple candidate root clusters representing low-pathology states (clusters 10, 12, 18), as well as high-pathology controls (clusters 21, 22). Across all tested roots:
- Spearman correlation between pseudotime and Braak stage was negligible (ρ ≈ 0).
- No consistent monotonic relationship was observed.
- UMAP visualization revealed local gradients without global alignment to pathology.
Branch-specific analyses detected only weak and non-reproducible local gradients. Therefore, EC is best described using a primarily state-based framework, while acknowledging limited local continuous structure in selected subgraphs. These results suggest that EC does not exhibit a global, disease-aligned trajectory, despite maintaining a connected graph structure.

Step 8. CellRank Check (optional)

CellRank was used as an additional directionality check to test whether EC supports a stable transition from low- to high-pathology states.

CellRank

To assess whether EC cells follow a directional progression toward pathological states, we applied CellRank using a connectivity-based transition model with low- and high-pathology states defined as terminal endpoints. Cells exhibited only a weak bias toward high-pathology states (Spearman ρ ≈ 0.21), and this bias did not translate into a coherent global gradient. Moreover, substantial overlap in fate probabilities across Braak stages indicates that pathological commitment is not aligned with a continuous trajectory. Together, these results do not support a model of linear disease progression in EC and instead suggest a distributed, state-based organization of pathology.

Step 9–10. Gene Programs and Module Scoring

Gene programs were defined from enrichment and visualized by module scoring.

programs · score_genes

Disease progression is organized as partially independent gene programs.
While trajectory inference methods typically assume a single coherent progression, our results indicate that disease progression is composed of multiple partially independent gene programs. These programs, including migration, stress response, and myelination, exhibit distinct and non-synchronous dynamics across pathology-associated states. Importantly, these programs do not align along a single axis, leading to a fragmented transition structure that cannot be captured by standard pseudotime or Markov-based approaches.

HC — Hippocampus

Discrete state architecture with strong excitatory-neuron-specific disease signal.
Detailed regional report

Step 1–2. Unsupervised Landscape and Disease Mapping

HC shows heterogeneous disease localization across multiple transcriptional islands.

UMAP · PAGA · pathology

Disease mapping across clusters in the hippocampus indicates that AD pathology is not organized along a continuous progression axis. Instead, clusters can be stratified into discrete disease-associated and low-pathology states based on AD enrichment and Braak score. While some clusters show higher pathology burden than others, these states do not form a consistent global ordering across the manifold. Neither AD fraction nor Braak score displays a coherent monotonic progression, and disease-associated clusters are distributed across multiple disconnected regions of the PAGA graph. Together, these results indicate that disease progression in HC is not represented as a single global trajectory in transcriptional space, but rather as a set of spatially separated, pathology-associated states.

Step 3–4. Structure Validation and Model Choice

HC was repeatedly re-checked with multiple roots and local branches to test the trajectory hypothesis.

DPT

To determine whether disease organization in the hippocampus follows a continuous trajectory or a discrete state-based structure, we performed targeted analyses of locally connected PAGA branches. We examined two representative modules:
Vasc/Epithelia branch (clusters 10–18–16) with moderate variation in pathology
Oligodendrocyte branch (clusters 0–9–1–24) representing a large, coherent cell population
For each branch, diffusion pseudotime (DPT) was computed using alternative root clusters to assess robustness.
In the Vasc/Epithelia branch, pseudotime showed weak and inconsistent correlation with Braak stage (Spearman ρ ≈ 0.08 and −0.24), and its structure was highly dependent on root choice, indicating instability and lack of a meaningful progression axis.
In the oligodendrocyte branch, pseudotime appeared stable across roots but showed no association with pathology (ρ ≈ 0.05). The inferred ordering reflected spatial structure in the embedding rather than biologically relevant progression.
Across both branches, Braak stage and AD diagnosis remained heterogeneous and did not align with pseudotime gradients. We found no evidence for either global or local continuous trajectories in HC. Instead, the data consistently support a state-based model, where disease-associated variation is captured by discrete transcriptional states rather than a unified progression axis.

Step 5–7. Disease-Associated States, Candidate Drivers and Cell-Type Interpretation

Driver analysis was performed globally and then refined in excitatory neurons to separate composition effects from state-specific signal.

global + Exc-specific

To characterize disease-associated transcriptional changes in HC, we defined discrete cellular states based on clusters enriched for low and high pathological burden.

Clusters with low AD prevalence and low Braak stage were assigned as low_pathology, while clusters enriched for AD and higher Braak stages were labeled as high_pathology. Remaining clusters were classified as intermediate.

This classification was supported by clear differences in pathological burden across states: low_pathology cells exhibited minimal AD enrichment and lower Braak stages, whereas high_pathology cells showed increased AD prevalence and higher Braak scores.

Importantly, disease-associated states were strongly associated with cell-type composition. Low_pathology states were predominantly composed of vascular and epithelial cells, whereas high_pathology states were enriched for excitatory neurons and astrocytes, indicating that disease-associated transcriptional programs are cell-type specific. Projection of disease states onto the PAGA graph revealed that these states are not arranged along a continuous trajectory. Instead, low- and high-pathology clusters occupy distinct regions of the graph, with no clear transitional path connecting them.

Cell-type-specific analysis (Excitatory neurons)
To isolate true disease-associated transcriptional changes, we performed the same analysis within excitatory neurons. This revealed a distinct set of genes upregulated in high_pathology Exc cells, including PTPRD, PLCG2, CHRM3, EFNA5, SORCS1, and MEF2C, which are associated with synaptic signaling, neuronal plasticity, and neurodegenerative processes. In contrast, genes upregulated in low_pathology Exc cells were enriched for more homeostatic and regulatory functions. Importantly, expression of high_pathology-associated genes showed a graded pattern across disease states (low → intermediate → high), indicating progressive activation rather than binary switching.

Step 9–10. Gene Programs and Module Scoring (Exc-focused)

Gene program analysis in excitatory neurons revealed a clear separation between disease-associated and homeostatic transcriptional states.

High-pathology excitatory neurons were enriched for programs related to neuronal structural remodeling, including axonogenesis, synaptic adhesion, and cytoskeletal dynamics. These were accompanied by activation of stress-response pathways, including HSF1-mediated transcription and chaperone-mediated autophagy, as well as pathways directly linked to Alzheimer’s disease pathology, such as regulation of amyloid-beta formation and tau kinase activity.

In contrast, low-pathology excitatory neurons were enriched for transcriptional regulatory programs, including RNA polymerase activity, chromatin remodeling, and key regulatory pathways involving TP53, MECP2, and PTEN, reflecting a homeostatic neuronal state.

Together, these results support a model in which excitatory neurons in the hippocampus transition between discrete transcriptional states characterized by either homeostatic regulation or disease-associated structural remodeling and stress responses.

PFC — Prefrontal cortex

Switch-like disease activation with strong high-pathology program engagement.
Detailed regional report

Step 1–2. Unsupervised Landscape and Disease Mapping

PFC displays clear island-like topology, with pathology occupying discrete transcriptional regions.

UMAP · PAGA · pathology

AD pathology shows a heterogeneous distribution across transcriptional states in the PFC. Clusters 0 and 13 show the strongest disease association and are oligodendrocyte-dominated, indicating strong involvement of oligodendrocyte states. Several additional clusters display moderate pathology and are primarily excitatory neurons, while others show lower AD burden, representing earlier or less affected states. Most clusters are highly cell-type-specific, suggesting that disease-associated states largely align with canonical cell types.

Step 3–4. Structure Validation and Model Choice

PFC does not show a continuous progression structure. Pathology is unevenly distributed across clusters and does not follow a coherent trajectory in the PAGA graph.

High-pathology clusters form localized branches, while others are scattered without a clear gradient.

Conclusion: PFC represents a multi-state landscape rather than a trajectory.

Model choice: State-based framework was selected. Analysis focuses on discrete disease-associated states rather than pseudotime trajectories.

Step 5–7. Disease-Associated States, Candidate Drivers and Cell-Type Interpretation

High-pathology states: 0, 13
Low-pathology states: 2, 3, 7, 12, 24

state-specific drivers

Differential expression analysis between high- and low-pathology states identified a robust set of genes upregulated in high-pathology states, including CTNNA3, ST18, PIP4K2A, MBP, SLC44A1, RNF220, PLP1 and ELMO1. These genes showed strong enrichment in high-pathology states and were consistently visualized across dotplot and heatmap analyses.

Additional correlation analysis with Braak stage revealed that several of these genes, including ST18, PLP1, ENPP2, DOCK5, SLC24A2, C10orf90, and FRMD4B, also increased with pathological burden, supporting their relevance as candidate pathology-associated drivers.

Overall, the PFC high-pathology states are characterized by a strong oligodendrocyte-enriched transcriptional program, suggesting that AD-related variation in this region is associated with cell-type-resolved molecular remodeling rather than a single global trajectory.

Step 8. CellRank Check (optional)

CellRank was used as an additional directionality check to test whether PFC supports a stable transition from low- to high-pathology states.

CellRank

Distribution of fate probabilities across Braak stages revealed no systematic increase with pathological burden. Median and overall distributions remained comparable across all stages, indicating the absence of a monotonic relationship between inferred fate probabilities and disease progression.

Step 9–10. Gene Programs and Module Scoring

PFC programs strongly emphasize myelination, axon-related processes, migration and signaling in high pathology.

switch-like programs

Gene program dynamics revealed a coordinated and abrupt activation of multiple transcriptional programs in high-pathology states. All programs, including myelination, cytoskeletal remodeling, axonal, and signaling modules, showed low and comparable activity in both low- and intermediate-pathology states, followed by a sharp increase specifically in high-pathology cells. This pattern is inconsistent with a gradual or sequential activation of programs along a continuous trajectory. Instead, it indicates a synchronized, switch-like transition into a distinct pathological state.
